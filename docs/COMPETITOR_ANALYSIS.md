# Competitor Analysis

*Why no existing tool solves the problem August Session solves.*

---

## The Distinction

Every tool analyzed below solves some form of the **information problem** — how to capture, organize, search, or retrieve knowledge.

None of them solve the **continuity problem** — how to return to an interrupted state of attention.

These are fundamentally different problems. Information tools ask: *"Where did I put that?"* Continuity tools ask: *"What was I doing?"*

August Session answers the second question with three things: your words, your desk, and a way to reopen it.

---

## Obsidian

**What it is.** A local-first note-taking application built around interlinked Markdown files. Emphasizes bidirectional linking, graph visualization, and a plugin ecosystem.

**What it solves.** Long-term knowledge organization. Building a personal "second brain" of connected ideas.

**Why it doesn't solve continuity.** Obsidian captures *what you know*. It does not capture *where you were*. You can write beautiful, interlinked notes about your research, but when you return tomorrow, Obsidian cannot tell you which five browser tabs you had open, what you were about to try, or what your screen looked like when you left. Your notes might remind you of the subject — but not the *state* of being mid-work.

Obsidian asks you to write for the archive. August asks you to write for tomorrow morning.

**The gap.** A sentence written in August at the moment of leaving — *"Check if the 2019 data contradicts the model"* — does more for resumption than a page of well-organized notes, because it was written by your brain while immersed in the work. It's a message from the inside.

---

## Notion

**What it is.** A cloud-based workspace for documents, databases, wikis, and project tracking. Highly flexible and collaborative.

**What it solves.** Collaborative documentation. Structured project tracking. Team knowledge bases.

**Why it doesn't solve continuity.** Notion organizes *information about work*. It does not capture *the experience of working*. You can have a perfectly maintained Notion workspace and still spend fifteen minutes every morning remembering what you were doing yesterday — because Notion knows your tasks, but not your context.

Notion is where you describe your work. August is where you leave a breadcrumb for yourself at the door.

**The gap.** Notion requires you to maintain structure — pages, databases, properties, views. August requires nothing. One hotkey, one sentence, one photograph. No organization. No maintenance. No structure. Just a moment, captured.

---

## Microsoft Recall

**What it is.** A Windows feature that periodically screenshots your screen and makes the history searchable using on-device processing.

**What it solves.** Retroactive search. "Find that thing I was looking at last Tuesday."

**Why it doesn't solve continuity.** Recall captures *everything* passively. It creates a haystack, then gives you a search engine. But continuity is not a search problem. When you sit down in the morning, you don't know what to search for — that's the whole problem. You've forgotten what you were doing. You need the answer to appear without a question.

Recall watches you. August listens to you. Recall records what happened. August records what you *wanted to remember*.

**The gap.** Recall has no concept of a "moment of leaving." It treats all screenshots equally. August privileges the one moment that matters — the pause — and attaches your own words to it. A screenshot alone is a picture. A screenshot with your words is a bridge.

---

## ActivityWatch

**What it is.** An open-source time tracker that records which applications and websites you use, for how long.

**What it solves.** Self-awareness about time usage. Knowing where your hours went.

**Why it doesn't solve continuity.** ActivityWatch tells you *that* you spent two hours in Google Docs yesterday. It doesn't tell you *what* you were doing, *why*, or *what you planned to do next*. It's a rearview mirror for time, not a bridge for attention.

Tracking activity is passive observation. Capturing a moment of pause is active intention.

**The gap.** ActivityWatch generates data. August generates recognition. Data requires analysis. Recognition is instant. You see your words, you see your desk, and you say "ah, right." No charts needed.

---

## Memex

**What it is.** A browser extension for bookmarking, annotating, and organizing web pages. Inspired by Vannevar Bush's original Memex concept.

**What it solves.** Web research organization. Saving and annotating interesting pages. Building collections of web content.

**Why it doesn't solve continuity.** Memex captures *content you found interesting*. It does not capture *the state of being in the middle of finding it*. You can have a well-organized Memex collection and still not remember which of those fifty saved pages you were actively comparing yesterday, or what question you were trying to answer.

Memex is a library. August is a bookmark in the book you were reading.

**The gap.** Memex requires curation — you choose what to save, tag it, organize it. August requires almost nothing — press a hotkey, write one sentence, leave. The effort difference is the point. Five seconds of effort at the right moment (when you're leaving) is worth more than five minutes of curation after the fact.

---

## DEVONthink

**What it is.** A powerful document management system for macOS. Stores, organizes, and searches documents of all types with built-in classification.

**What it solves.** Document management at scale. Finding files across large personal archives. Connecting related documents.

**Why it doesn't solve continuity.** DEVONthink is a warehouse for documents. It knows where every file is. But it doesn't know what you were *doing* with those files yesterday. It can find your PDF — but not the fact that you were reading page 47 while comparing it with a spreadsheet on the right side of your screen and a browser tab with the original source on the left.

DEVONthink organizes what you have. August remembers where you were.

**The gap.** DEVONthink solves the problem of "I can't find that document." August solves the problem of "I can't find my train of thought." These are different problems. You can find every document and still be lost. You can find no documents but read your own words — *"Compare Table 3 with the 2019 figures"* — and know exactly what to do.

## Summary

| Tool | Solves | Asks | Requires |
|---|---|---|---|
| Obsidian | Knowledge organization | "Where did I write that?" | Ongoing curation |
| Notion | Collaborative documentation | "What's the status?" | Structural maintenance |
| Microsoft Recall | Retroactive search | "Find that thing" | Nothing (passive) — but also gives nothing without a query |
| ActivityWatch | Time awareness | "Where did my time go?" | Nothing (passive) — but answers a different question |
| Memex | Web research collection | "Where did I save that?" | Active saving and tagging |
| DEVONthink | Document management | "Where is that file?" | Import and classification |
| **August Session** | **Continuity of attention** | **"What was I doing?"** | **One sentence when you leave** |

Every tool in this list is useful. None of them are what August Session is.

They solve information. August solves continuity.

They help you find things. August helps you find *yourself* — the version of you that was here yesterday, mid-thought, about to do something important.

The bridge is your words, your desk, and a way back.

Nothing else is needed.
